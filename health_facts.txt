Adults aged 18-64 need 7 to 9 hours of sleep per night according to the National Sleep Foundation.
Chronic sleep of under 6 hours per night is associated with higher risks of cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and impaired immune function.
The World Health Organization recommends at least 150-300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week for adults.
Alternatively, the WHO recommends 75-150 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity per week for adults.
Muscle-strengthening activities involving all major muscle groups should be done on 2 or more days a week.
A resting heart rate between 60 and 100 bpm is considered normal for most adults.
Well-trained athletes often have resting heart rates as low as 40-60 bpm, which is healthy for them.
A consistently elevated resting heart rate above 90 bpm can signal stress, dehydration, illness, or poor recovery.
The American Heart Association suggests a target exercise heart rate zone of 50-85% of maximum heart rate (roughly 220 minus age).
Walking 7,000 to 10,000 steps per day is associated with substantially reduced all-cause mortality risk in adults.
Even an increase from 4,000 to 7,000 steps per day can yield major health benefits, especially for sedentary individuals.
Daily caloric needs for most adults range from 1,800 to 2,500 kcal, depending on age, sex, body size, and activity level.
Active adults may burn 2,200-3,000 kcal per day; sedentary adults often burn closer to 1,600-2,000 kcal.
Hydration guidelines suggest roughly 2.7 L of total water per day for women and 3.7 L for men, including water from food.
Dehydration of as little as 1-2% of body weight can impair cognitive performance, mood, and physical endurance.
Sleep debt accumulated during the week is not fully recovered by sleeping in on weekends; consistency matters more than catch-up.
Exposure to bright natural light within 30 minutes of waking helps regulate circadian rhythm and improves nighttime sleep quality.
Avoiding caffeine within 8-10 hours of bedtime can meaningfully improve sleep onset and depth.
Alcohol disrupts REM sleep and typically lowers overall sleep quality even if it helps you fall asleep faster.
Short naps of 10-20 minutes can boost alertness without causing sleep inertia or interfering with night-time sleep.
Active recovery days (light walking, stretching, mobility work) often improve long-term progress more than full rest days.
A protein intake of about 1.2-1.6 g per kg of body weight supports muscle recovery for regularly active adults.
Breaking up long sitting periods with 2-3 minutes of light movement every 30 minutes improves cardiometabolic markers.
Chronic stress raises resting heart rate and cortisol, and is correlated with reduced heart rate variability (HRV).
Higher HRV generally indicates better autonomic nervous system recovery and cardiovascular fitness.
A sudden drop in HRV or spike in resting HR by 5-10 bpm can indicate incoming illness, overtraining, or poor sleep.
Deep breathing for 5 minutes (e.g., 4-second inhale, 6-second exhale) can lower heart rate and perceived stress.
Eating within a 10-12 hour daily window (time-restricted eating) may improve metabolic markers in some adults.
Going to bed and waking up at roughly the same time every day — within ~30 minutes — strongly improves sleep quality.
After poor sleep, reducing high-intensity workouts and prioritizing a walk, sunlight, and an earlier bedtime aids recovery.
